Planning About

Oviedo

Day 3

In the morning we packed up and headed to our bus for Oviedo. The scenery is absolutely breathtaking. I’m sure everyone around us thinks we are those ridiculous tourists who won’t put the camera away. Oviedo doesn’t have foreign tourists, so Spanish is the only language we could use. When I was in college I did a study abroad here for a month. We came here to visit my former host, Nieves. She also introduced us to her two friends, Pepita and Sole. These three became our hosts for the next 5 days.
Pepita picked us up from the bus station. She asked if I had been practicing Spanish and I told her no, so we kept the conversation on simple words. We had another yummy chocolate con churros and met our hostess Sole. Sole is so cool-she is very easy going and very nice. She took us to the apartment we’d be staying at.
They have a very hard time saying the word “Trent” so first they called him Trench, but now they call him Tres. This makes it very confusing when trying to follow the conversation because I don't know if they are saying Trent or the number three. Dinner with Nieves, Pepita, and Sole was fabulous because they could order the food for us. They call it pinchos (basically a bunch if hors d’oeuvres that everyone around the table shares).

Day 4

It has been raining nonstop since we got here. We went to the Auditorio Principe Felipe to watch an orchestra concert-The Orchestra of Oviedo and two famous Spanish singers. The difference being, at the end in the U.S. you would stand up and applaud for an encore. Here it is the opposite. You stay seated and clap-but there were 3 encore pieces. (I may have taken a nap during one of the pieces.) After we went for tapas. The culture here is to drink a lot of coffee. Likewise Trent and I are getting very good at ordering fanta limon and naranja, agua, and chocolate con churros when we stop at cafes. We're also getting very good at spotting all the bathrooms too.

Day 5

Today was a very busy day. Sole and Pepita took us for an all day drive to see the rest of Austurias. Austurias is like a state in Spain and Oviedo is the capital.
We started at noon with coffee for them and drove to Congas de Onis. This was the first government city for Austurias. There is an old bridge here that has the Alpha and Omega symbols. All throughout Austurias, they are very proud of their Catholic heritage. This is because the Moorish people never conquered this area because it is protected by mountains.
One thing about the drive, the area is very beautiful. They believe in working with the land, not exploiting it. We drove through many tunnels in the mountains. They build the road through, not around so the landscape looks natural. The highways blend in so well that sometimes it is hard to spot them out. One tunnel even goes under a river. Sole says it’s because the ecologist didn’t approve of a bridge going over.
After a coffee break, we went to Covadonga. This town has a basilica and a church built into a cave. If I remember right, this is the place they say that Saint Mary came and threw rocks down on the Moorish army, and this is the spot that the Spanish Christians started to gain their land back. But the thing I was most excited about - there is a fountain with 12 spouts and the story is if you drink from it, (which I did last time I was here), then you'll get married in a year. Hence why I scored Trent so fast. I think Trent liked to see where the spell had been cast on him!
Our next stop was Ribadesella-our first matter of business-coffee! And three o’clock lunch. We then toured Covadonga. There is a Capilla (a small church) at the top of a hill where you can see the city and the sea. We got some great pictures there. And I know Trent loved his ocean view. Then it was down the hill and onto another cup of coffee. No wonder people here never look tired.
Our three hosts are so great to us. They keep paying for our food even though we try to pay. The last coffee break Trent was determined to pay. We ate outside so after we got our drinks, Trent went in pretending to go to the bathroom, but Pepita followed him in. A few minutes later he came back out with the saddest defeated face I've ever seen and said "Pepita habla espanol y yo no hablo espanol". Looks like we lost out on another chance to pay the bill.
We then got in the car and saw probably one of the coolest natural formations. Nearby there is a cave that goes all the way through a mountain. It was formed by a river. Now they built a rode so you can actually drive through it. So cool! Last we stopped at Lastres where Pepita’s husband is buried.
Roads in the countryside are very narrow and barely ideal for one car. But everyone likes to drive fast, so when you make a turn around the corner, you just honk to alert any other car coming the opposite direction. Our drives consisted of much honking intertwined with abrupt halts every so often when we encountered other cars.
After an 8 hour tour we were back at our apartment. We enjoyed seeing much of the countryside but were exhausted. With it being a Friday-we still had to go out, the nightlife is great here. We walked around the city and ate a few Tapas. It’s a prideful moment every time we successfully order food on our own. Typically nightlife starts at 9 or 10 and people stay up til 3,4 or 5 am. We lasted til 11 and called it quits. The lifestyle here is to wake up at 10 or 11am and stay out way later. It’s any teenagers dream. Even little kids stay out late.

Day 6

We took a bus to see Gijon for a few hours. Nieves said it was only a 20 min. ride. Unfortunately, we somehow managed to get on the bus that stops at EVERY LITTLE TOWN between Oviedo and Gijon. At first it was frustrating, and then we just laughed as we would cross the freeway, not to get on for a straight shot, but to head into another little town. About an hour later, we peaced out from the bus and were at Gijon. Finally at the beach, it was very relaxing to just watch the waves crash on the shore. But too soon it was time to go. We made sure to catch the direct bus on our way home – a truly 20 min. ride.
Earlier that day, there was an old fashion Austurias market. Everyone was dressed in costumes and they were selling things you would have seen one hundred years ago. There were people everywhere-maybe it was a holiday, but I’m not really sure. It’s funny how as a tourist you really don’t know what is going on. It’s that ignorance that seems to make you feel ok walking past graffiti walls-because for all you know, this could be the safest part of town.

Day 7

Being Sunday, we headed off to church. Now let me deviate to how people dress here. Let’s just say that today was the only day that we slightly looked like we blended in. People dress top notch here. Even the children do. I have yet to see a little girl under the age of 3 in nothing less than a dress. As Trent put it-and it’s pretty accurate “Where are all the babies in Tuxedos going?”
We bought flowers for Nieves, Pepita, and Sole from the market as today is mother’s day. We got in Pepita’s car and drove to Sole’s house. Sole lives in the countryside in a house with her mother. We had lunch outside. It was awesome! We felt so comfortable like we were locals.
Ok now for a time that really got lost in translation. Pepita asked me if I wanted a banana –yes! Then they asked if we wanted to go on a walk- so now I'm thinking we have to walk to the banana. Next thing we know they are bringing out hiking shoes and straw hats for us. (I guess our get up wasn't good enough to chance upon this enchanting banana tree). Anyway so then come out the walking sticks and we start climbing straight up the mountain into a dried up mud slide. We climbed for 30 minutes to nothing turned around and hiked back down-at the end Trent and I couldn't stop laughing at whatever we had just agreed to and still no banana!
We then drove back to the city to Nieves' house. And guess what was waiting for us there? Bananas! What a fabulous reward for our confusion.
